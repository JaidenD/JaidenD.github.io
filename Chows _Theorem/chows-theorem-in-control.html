Chow's Theorem

Note that this is work in progress.

In this note we will first build up to Chow’s theorem and then discuss its implications in physics and control.

Let \(\mathcal D\) be a \(C^\infty\)-distribution over a base manifold \(M\). We wish to leave open the possibility of \(\mathcal D\) having non-constant rank among points on \(M\), hence we view \(\mathcal D\) as a sheaf of vector fields rather than as a subbundle of \(TM\). We use \(\Gamma^\infty(\mathcal D)\) to denote smooth sections of \(\mathcal D\).

In classical differential equation theory the central problem is to find integral curves of a vector field. The natural generalization of this idea is to find integral manifolds of distributions. We will quickly outline this idea and state a classical result. For simplicity, we will work purely with smooth objects.

A local integral manifold through \(x_0\) of \(\mathcal D\) is an immersed submanifold \(S\) of a neighborhood \(U\) of \(x_0\) such that, for all \(x \in S\), \[ T_xS \subseteq \mathcal D_x. \] A local integral manifold is maximal if \[ T_xS = \mathcal D_x \] for all \(x \in S\). If a maximal local integral manifold for \(\mathcal D\) containing \(x\) contains all other maximal local integral manifolds through \(x\), then it is said to be a maximal integral manifold. The distribution \(\mathcal D\) is called integrable if every point has a maximal integral manifold through it.

We now introduce a seemingly unrelated idea: involutivity. The distribution \(\mathcal D\) is said to be involutive if it is closed under the Lie bracket, \[ [X,Y] = X(Y)-Y(X). \] That is, \(\mathcal D\) forms a Lie algebra.

Surprisingly, these two ideas are beautifully related by the classical Frobenius theorem:

\[ \boxed{ \mathcal D \text{ is integrable} \iff \mathcal D \text{ is involutive} } \]

By Zorn’s lemma, one can also show that given a distribution \(\mathcal D\), there exists a minimal involutive distribution containing \(\mathcal D\), called the involutive closure of \(\mathcal D\). We denote it by \(\mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)\). Accepting this lemma without proof for now, we show how to construct \(\mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)\).

Recursively define \[ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lie}^{(l)}(\mathcal D)_x &= \mathrm{Lie}^{(l-1)}(\mathcal D)_x \\ &\quad + \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R} \left\{ [X,Y](x) : X \in \Gamma^\infty\!\left( \mathrm{Lie}^{(l_1)}(\mathcal D) \right), \right. \\ &\qquad\qquad\qquad\left. Y \in \Gamma^\infty\!\left( \mathrm{Lie}^{(l_2)}(\mathcal D) \right), \; l_1+l_2=l-1 \right\}, \\ \mathrm{Lie}^{(0)}(\mathcal D)_x &= \mathcal D_x. \end{aligned} \]

By the previously mentioned lemma, this recursion becomes stationary pointwise for finite \(m(x)\): \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(n)}(\mathcal D)_x = \mathrm{Lie}^{(n+1)}(\mathcal D)_x, \qquad \forall n \geq m(x). \]

Hence, as the algebraists would say, the involutive closure of \(\mathcal D\) is obtained by taking the pointwise direct limit of \(\mathrm{Lie}^{(l)}(\mathcal D)\): \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)_x = \varinjlim_l \mathrm{Lie}^{(l)}(\mathcal D)_x. \]

Switching gears for a moment, we can also use \(\mathcal D\) to generate a set of diffeomorphisms on the base \(M\). Let \(\Phi_t^X\) be the time-\(t\) flow generated by the vector field \(X\). Define \(\mathrm{Diff}(\mathcal D)\) to be the set of diffeomorphisms of \(M\) of the form \[ \Phi_{t_1}^{X_1} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_{t_k}^{X_k}, \qquad t_1,\dots,t_k \in \mathbb R, \quad X_1,\dots,X_k \in \Gamma^\infty(\mathcal D), \quad k \in \mathbb N. \] Note that this definition allows negative-time flows along a vector field.

Define the orbit of a point \(x_0\) by \[ \mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D) = \left\{ \phi(x_0) : \phi \in \mathrm{Diff}(\mathcal D) \right\}. \]

In isolation, this object would be pretty horrible to compute, since one would have to compute the flows of \(k\) vector fields. However, amazingly, we can describe the orbit set using \(\mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)\).

Orbit Theorem.

Let \(\mathcal D\) be a smooth distribution on \(M\), and let \(x_0 \in M\). Then the following statements hold:

  1. \(\mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D)\) is a smooth immersed submanifold.
  2. For each \(x \in \mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D)\), \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)_x \subseteq T_x \mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D). \]
  3. If \(x \sim y\) means \(y \in \mathcal O(x,\mathcal D)\), then \[ M = \bigsqcup_{[x] \in M/{\sim}} \mathcal O(x,\mathcal D). \]

Note that if the distribution were analytic rather than smooth, then the inclusion in point 2 would be an equality. To illustrate this, we show an example of a smooth distribution where this inclusion is strict.

Let \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} e^{-1/x^2}, & x \neq 0, \\ 0, & x = 0. \end{cases} \] You may remember from your analysis class that \(f\) is smooth but not analytic.

Define a smooth distribution \[ \mathcal D = \operatorname{span}\{X,Y\}, \qquad X = \partial_x, \qquad Y = f(x)\partial_y. \]

Since \(Y(0,y)=0\), we have \[ \mathcal D_{(0,y)} = \operatorname{span}\{\partial_x\}. \] Computing the first Lie bracket gives \[ [X,Y] = f'(x)\partial_y. \]

Acting by \(X\) \(k\) times via the Lie bracket gives \(k\) nested Lie brackets, denoted \(\mathrm{ad}_X^k(\cdot)\), so computing higher brackets gives \[ \mathrm{ad}_X^k(Y) = f^{(k)}(x)\partial_y. \]

In particular, \(f\) is flat at zero, so \[ f^{(k)}(0)=0 \qquad \text{for all } k \geq 0. \] Therefore every iterated Lie bracket evaluated at \((0,y)\) gives no \(\partial_y\)-direction. Hence \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)_{(0,y)} = \operatorname{span}\{\partial_x\}. \]

However, the orbit through \((0,y)\) is two-dimensional. Start at \((0,y)\), then flow along \(X\) to \((\varepsilon,y)\) with \(\varepsilon \neq 0\). At this new point, \[ Y(\varepsilon,y) = f(\varepsilon)\partial_y \neq 0. \] Thus, one can move in the \(y\)-direction and then flow back along \(X\) to \(x=0\). The net action of this flow is a translation along the \(y\)-direction despite the Lie brackets at \(x=0\) missing the direction.

Therefore, \[ T_{(0,y)} \mathcal O((0,y),\mathcal D) = T_{(0,y)}\mathbb R^2 = \operatorname{span}\{\partial_x,\partial_y\}, \] while \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)_{(0,y)} = \operatorname{span}\{\partial_x\}. \] Thus we get the strict inclusion \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)_x \subset T_x\mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D). \]

We now arrive at Chow’s theorem as a corollary of the orbit theorem.

Chow’s Theorem.

If \(M\) is connected and if, for a \(C^\infty\)-distribution \(\mathcal D\) on \(M\), \[ \mathrm{Lie}^{(\infty)}(\mathcal D)=TM, \] then \[ \mathcal O(x_0,\mathcal D)=M. \]
